\begin{abstract}
Why does associational life so often fail to produce generalized trust in unequal societies? We argue that the answer lies not in participation alone, but in the configuration and durability of memberships across social domains. Cross-domain portfolios are most conducive to generalized trust, yet they are also the hardest to sustain. By contrast, memberships clustered within a single domain can support localized trust without extending it to strangers. We show that generalized trust is highest among individuals with cross-domain memberships, lower among the isolated, and lowest among those in clustered portfolios. For neighborhood trust, however, clustered participation performs much better, approaching the levels associated with cross-domain memberships. We further show that cross-domain portfolios are the least stable over time, and that their persistence is socially stratified in ways consistent with structural precarity rather than a generic resource advantage. Under inequality, the civil society--trust gap reflects not a lack of participation, but the difficulty of sustaining the forms of participation most likely to generate generalized trust.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

%% --- Paragraph 1: Opening with empirical tension ---
Societies with dense associational landscapes do not consistently exhibit high interpersonal trust. Cross-national evidence shows that the relationship between civic participation and generalized trust is heterogeneous: associations that create connections across socially distinct groups are more consistently associated with trust than associations concentrated within homogeneous ones, but even this qualification leaves substantial variation unexplained \parencite{paxton_social_2002, paxton_association_2007, stolle1998all}. Chile sharpens the puzzle. The country has approximately 300,000 registered voluntary associations \parencite{irarrazaval_chile_2017}---a large associational infrastructure by regional standards---yet generalized trust stands below 8\% and has declined over the past decade despite stable associational density. The standard response invokes type: what matters is not how many associations exist but whether they create cross-cutting ties \parencite{putnam_bowling_2000, coleman_social_1988}. This response is partially correct but leaves a deeper question unanswered. If connective participation generates trust \parencite{paxton_association_2007}, why does a society with abundant civil society systematically fail to produce it? We argue the answer lies not in the quantity or type of associations but in a structural condition that the type-based account does not address: the capacity to reproduce, over time, the configurations of membership through which trust generalization becomes possible.

%% --- Paragraph 2: Diagnosis — structural reproduction, not measurement ---
We argue that this gap reflects a structural condition that is endogenous to civil society under inequality, not an artifact of how participation is measured. Prior research typically operationalizes civic engagement as a count of memberships, treating affiliations as interchangeable units regardless of how they are distributed across organizational domains \parencite{coleman_social_1988, putnam_bowling_2000}. This additive approach conceals a consequential distinction. Portfolios concentrated within a single domain---such as neighborhood or religious organizations---generate repeated interaction within bounded circles. Portfolios spanning multiple domains expose individuals to heterogeneous organizational settings and, through that exposure, create conditions under which trust expectations can extend beyond familiar groups \parencite{simmel_conflict_1922, breiger_duality_1974}. Critically, this distinction operates at the individual portfolio level, not at the level of organizational type. Whether an organization is connective depends partly on the other memberships its members hold: an individual who belongs simultaneously to a neighborhood association, a professional body, and a charitable organization creates cross-domain connections that no single organization can generate alone \parencite{breiger_duality_1974, mcpherson_hypernetwork_1982}. The relevant unit of analysis is therefore not the organization but the membership portfolio. Cross-domain portfolios are not simply more memberships; they constitute a qualitatively different structural position in the associational field. The critical problem is not that civil society lacks these configurations---it is that, under conditions of material inequality, the associational field fails to reproduce them. Because cross-domain participation imposes higher maintenance costs than within-domain participation, and because those costs are unequally distributed, the configurations most conducive to generalized trust are also the most fragile. Civil society can exhibit organizational abundance while failing to sustain integrative capacity \parencite{portes_social_1998, oxhorn_neopluralism_2004}.

%% --- Paragraph 3: Mechanism — structural precarity (clean definition) ---
We refer to this condition as \textit{structural precarity}: the systematic fragility of the membership configurations most conducive to generalized trust under conditions of material inequality. The concept has a specific causal logic. Cross-domain participation requires coordinating commitments across organizations with incompatible schedules, navigating heterogeneous organizational cultures, and sustaining effort across settings that offer different social rewards \parencite{simmel_conflict_1922}. These coordination demands are not equally distributed across the population. Individuals with fewer economic resources, less flexible employment, and greater caregiving responsibilities face higher effective costs of maintaining cross-domain portfolios \parencite{oxhorn_neopluralism_2004, cook2005cooperation}. Under persistent inequality, this asymmetry means that exits from bridging configurations are socially patterned rather than random: those who can least afford the maintenance costs of cross-domain participation are the first to leave. As bridging individuals exit, the cross-domain exposure through which trust generalization operates contracts---not because associations disappear, but because the individuals who sustained integrative connections across them no longer do \parencite{wellman_community_1979, fukuyama1996trust}. More broadly, structural precarity points to a general mechanism through which inequality reproduces itself via civil society: by making the configurations most conducive to generalized trust the hardest to sustain, inequality limits not only economic mobility but the social conditions under which cooperation
across groups becomes possible \parencite{cook2005cooperation, fukuyama1996trust}.

%% --- Paragraph 4: Empirical signature — the SES gradient reversal ---
Structural precarity generates a prediction that standard resource accounts
of civic participation cannot accommodate. If socioeconomic advantage enables participation broadly and uniformly, the SES gradient should operate in the same direction across all portfolio types: more educated individuals should join more organizations, sustain more commitments, and exit fewer, regardless of configuration \parencite{paxton_association_2007,
paxton_social_2002}. Our data contradict this prediction in a theoretically consequential way. Higher education predicts \textit{retention} in cross-domain bridging portfolios---but simultaneously predicts \textit{higher exit} from isolated and clustered portfolios, consistent with upward mobility toward bridging rather than generic retention across positions. The direction of the SES gradient reverses depending on where individuals are positioned in the associational field. This reversal is the empirical signature that distinguishes structural precarity from a generic association between socioeconomic resources and participation. It implies that inequality does not simply suppress civic engagement: it concentrates fragility precisely where the trust-generating potential of civil society is highest \parencite{stolle1998all, cook2005cooperation}. A theory of resources alone cannot produce this prediction; a structural account of portfolio positions and their maintenance requirements can.

%% --- Paragraph 5: Framework, data, and design ---
To examine these arguments, we draw on Breiger's actor--group duality and Wellman's Community Question to formalize three portfolio positions in the associational field: associational isolation ($\alpha$), within-domain clustering ($\beta$), and cross-domain bridging ($\gamma$) \parencite{breiger_duality_1974, wellman_community_1979}. These positions correspond to Wellman's Community Lost, Saved, and Liberated configurations and imply distinct pathways to particularized versus generalized trust. We estimate these positions using latent Markov models applied to three waves of the Chilean Longitudinal Social Survey (ELSOC; 2016, 2018, 2022), following a balanced panel of 1{,}304 adults. Transition probabilities from the latent Markov model capture the stability of portfolio positions over time. We then relate time-varying positions to generalized and neighborhood trust using random-effects probit panel models with wave fixed effects, reporting results as average marginal effects to facilitate substantive interpretation.

%% --- Paragraph 6: Results with real numbers (all key findings anticipated) ---
Four results emerge. First, the three portfolio positions are empirically distinguishable and substantively interpretable. Position $\alpha$ (isolation) captures 55\% of respondents and displays uniformly low embership probabilities across all organizational domains. Position $\beta$ (clustering) captures 34\% and concentrates membership in neighborhood and religious organizations. Position $\gamma$ (bridging) captures only 11\% and shows elevated membership probabilities across heterogeneous domains---neighborhood, charitable, professional, and labor organizations simultaneously. Covariate patterns confirm the resource logic: higher education predicts occupying $\gamma$ over $\alpha$ (OR~$= 3.24$, $p < .001$), while unemployment predicts occupying $\alpha$ over $\gamma$ (OR~$= 2.18$, $p < .01$). Second, portfolio positions are differentially associated with trust in ways that reflect distinct trust radii---and the ordering across positions differs by trust type in ways the theory predicts. For generalized trust, the ordering is $\gamma > \alpha > \beta$: clustered individuals show \textit{lower} generalized trust than even isolated ones (AME$_\beta = -0.056$ vs.\ AME$_\alpha = -0.033$ relative to $\gamma$). This is not a null result or anomaly---it is the prediction that follows directly from Wellman's Community Saved configuration. Within-domain
clustering does not merely fail to generate generalized trust; it actively reinforces ingroup boundaries that constrain trust generalization beyond familiar circles, producing lower generalized trust than the absence of organizational embeddedness altogether \parencite{portes_social_1998,
stolle1998all}. For neighborhood trust, the ordering reverses to $\gamma > \beta > \alpha$---with $\beta$ only 2.2 points below $\gamma$ (indistinguishable from bridging) compared to 8.0 points for isolation---precisely because bounded interaction sustains localized but not generalized trust expectations. This double reversal across trust radii is the sharpest empirical confirmation of the configurational argument: the same organizational embeddedness that generates localized cooperation actively suppresses generalized trust. Third, bridging is the least stable position: the model-implied persistence probability is 0.31 for $\gamma$, compared to 0.61 for $\beta$ and 0.59 for $\alpha$. Because $\gamma$ is both rare (11\%) and unstable, the population-level distribution of cross-domain exposure is structurally thin and vulnerable to contraction. Fourth, and most consequentially, exits from $\gamma$ are socioeconomically patterned in the direction structural precarity predicts: higher education reduces exit probability from bridging by 27.7 percentage points, while among individuals in $\alpha$ and $\beta$, higher education \textit{increases} exit probability by 4.2 and 10.0 points respectively---consistent with upward mobility toward bridging rather than generic retention. The SES gradient reverses direction at exactly the position where trust-generating potential is highest, a result inconsistent with generic resource accounts and consistent with structural precarity. These individual-level patterns are consistent with the aggregate puzzle: when bridging portfolios are both rare and unstable, the population-level distribution of cross-domain exposure contracts, limiting the extent to which associational life translates into generalized trust at the aggregate level. We treat this field-level implication as descriptive rather than as a causal estimate of system-level change, and return to its scope conditions in the discussion.

%% --- Paragraph 7: Contribution (one sentence) ---
These findings reframe the civil society--trust relationship as a problem of structural reproduction: under inequality, the associational field fails to sustain the configurations most capable of generating generalized trust, and resolving this tension requires attending not to how many memberships individuals hold but to whether the portfolio positions that matter can be maintained across time \parencite{coleman_social_1988, putnam_bowling_2000,
paxton_association_2007}.

\section{Theoretical Framework: Multiple Embeddedness and Trust Formation}

\subsection{The Structural Role of Voluntary Associations}

Voluntary associations are institutionalized settings of interaction beyond family, school, and work, and they matter because they shape who encounters whom. By organizing social activity within specific foci, associations alter the probability of repeated contact and exposure across social categories \parencite{knoke_associations_1986, feld_focused_1981}. In this sense, associations can either reproduce bounded interaction or facilitate contact across otherwise separated social worlds, making them a plausible site for trust formation processes \parencite{blau_exchange_1986, mcpherson_hypernetwork_1982}.

Associations can influence trust through at least two pathways. First, they provide repeated interaction and shared expectations that can support cooperation among participants \parencite{glanville_voluntary_2004, paxton_trust_2018}. Second, they can connect individuals to resources and information that lie outside their immediate networks, especially when participation spans distinct organizational domains \parencite{lin_social_2002, diani_cement_2015}. These pathways imply that what matters is not only whether individuals participate, but also how their memberships are organized across organizational settings.

A key implication is that integration across civil society typically operates through individuals rather than through formal ties between organizations. Members who hold multiple affiliations create cross-domain connections that would otherwise be absent, effectively linking organizational settings through co-membership \parencite{diani_cement_2015, pachucki_cultural_2010}. This shifts the analytical focus from the attributes of single organizations to the configurations of memberships individuals assemble, which may be more informative for trust formation than participation counts alone \parencite{moody_structural_2003, paxton_association_2007}.


\subsection{Breiger's Actor-Group Duality: The Formal Foundation}

We build on Breiger's actor--group duality: individuals are connected through shared group memberships, and groups are connected through shared members \parencite{breiger_duality_1974}. This dual perspective clarifies why membership portfolios can generate emergent patterns that are not reducible to either individual traits or organizational attributes alone. In our setting, the key theoretical object is therefore the configuration of an individual's affiliations within an associational field.

Formally, we represent the associational field as a bipartite graph linking individuals to association types. Let $X$ denote the individual--association incidence matrix, where $x_{ia}=1$ if individual $i$ reports membership in association type $a$ and $0$ otherwise. This representation makes explicit that what we observe empirically are membership portfolios---rows of $X$---from which distinct positions in the associational field can be identified.

The same bipartite representation also implies two useful projections. Projecting onto association types yields $P_A = X^{T}X$, where entries summarize co-membership across domains; projecting onto individuals yields $P_I = XX^{T}$, where entries summarize shared affiliations. In this paper, we leverage these implications primarily to motivate why cross-domain portfolios can create integrative connections in the associational field, while our empirical identification focuses on portfolio patterns at the individual level.


\subsection{Wellman's Community Question: Theorizing Emergent Configurations}

Wellman's Community Question provides a typology of network configurations that can coexist within modern societies \parencite{wellman_community_1979}. Rather than treating ``community'' as simply lost or preserved, the framework distinguishes configurations that differ in how ties are clustered, how far they extend beyond local contexts, and what kinds of support they can plausibly provide. We use this typology to link membership portfolios to theoretically interpretable positions in an associational field.

\textbf{Community Lost} refers to configurations in which ties are sparse and weakly institutionalized, limiting access to support beyond immediate contingencies. In such settings, individuals participate in multiple, loosely connected networks that provide little reliable assistance and few opportunities for trust generalization beyond familiar circles.

\textbf{Community Saved} captures configurations dominated by dense, bounded clusters---often organized around neighborhood, kin, or other homogeneous foci---that support cooperation through repeated interaction and shared expectations. These settings can sustain localized trust and enforceable cooperation, but they may also limit exposure to heterogeneous others outside the cluster.

\textbf{Community Liberated} describes configurations in which meaningful ties persist but extend across settings, producing wider-reaching, more heterogeneous networks. These networks are less locally bounded and can provide access to differentiated resources and perspectives \parencite{fischer_dwell_1982}. In principle, this pattern is most compatible with generalized trust, because it involves sustained contact across social contexts rather than interaction confined to a single domain.

Wellman's typology specifies what these configurations look like, but it is less explicit about how they are produced within civil society. Our contribution is to treat patterns of multiple organizational embeddedness as one mechanism that can generate these configurations in an associational field. By focusing on membership portfolios, we translate Community Lost, Saved, and Liberated into empirically identifiable positions that differ in cross-domain exposure and, consequently, in their predicted relationship to trust.

\subsection{Small's Organizational Embeddedness: Within - Organization Processes}

Small's organizational embeddedness perspective highlights that organizations differ in the kinds of social relations they routinely produce \parencite{small_unanticipated_2009}. Some settings foster repeated interaction among the same participants, while others bring together more heterogeneous actors and facilitate weaker, more bridging forms of contact. These within-organization differences imply that participation can generate distinct social experiences even before considering multiple memberships.

Our framework extends this insight from within-organization processes to between-organization exposure. Individuals who participate across domains can combine distinct organizational settings into a broader portfolio of encounters, potentially generating forms of cross-cutting contact that no single organization can provide on its own. This is consistent with social capital arguments that heterogeneous ties expand access to information and opportunities \parencite{lin_social_2002}.

This portfolio perspective helps distinguish two theoretically relevant cases that are often conflated in additive measures. A person with multiple memberships concentrated within a single domain may accumulate repeated interaction and reinforcement within a bounded circle, whereas a person with memberships spanning domains is more likely to encounter heterogeneous others across settings. The distinction is central for our argument because it implies different pathways to localized versus generalized trust.

\subsection{Conceptualizing Trust Formation: From Particularized to Generalized}

To link membership portfolios to trust outcomes, we distinguish forms of trust that differ in scope. Some trust is particularized: it is anchored in known relationships and supported by information, reciprocity, and monitoring within bounded networks. Other trust is generalized: it extends to unknown others and is less dependent on direct knowledge of specific partners. This distinction matters because different associational positions plausibly generate trust at different radii.

Particularized trust can arise from repeated exchange and the accumulation of information about others' behavior \parencite{blau_exchange_1986, kollock_emergence_1994}. It can also be reinforced by network closure, where reputational sanctions and monitoring make defection costly \parencite{coleman_foundations_2000, portes_embeddedness_1993}. These mechanisms are most likely in clustered settings that concentrate interaction within a bounded community.

Generalized trust, by contrast, refers to a broader orientation toward strangers and unknown others \parencite{uslaner_moral_2001, hardin_conceptions_2001}. Because it is not grounded in repeated interaction with the same partners, generalized trust plausibly depends on experiences that support the extension of trust expectations beyond one's immediate circle. In civil society, this extension is more likely when participation exposes individuals to heterogeneous others across distinct settings.

We therefore expect different associational positions to relate to different trust radii. Positions characterized by within-domain clustering should be more conducive to localized trust, insofar as they intensify repeated interaction and closure-based enforcement. Positions characterized by cross-domain participation should be more conducive to generalized trust, insofar as they increase exposure to diverse organizational contexts and make trust expectations portable across settings \parencite{hedstrom_dissecting_2005, glanville_why_2016}. In our framework, this maps naturally onto Position $\beta$ (clustering) versus Position $\gamma$ (bridging).


\subsection{Operationalizing Wellman's Configurations Through Multiple Embeddedness Patterns}

We next translate Wellman's configurations into empirically identifiable positions defined by membership portfolios. The key idea is that patterns of multiple organizational embeddedness vary in both intensity (how many memberships) and breadth (how many distinct domains). These dimensions imply different levels of cross-domain exposure and, in turn, different expected relationships to generalized versus localized trust.

\textbf{Position $\alpha$ (Associational Isolation)} corresponds to minimal organizational embeddedness. Formally, individuals in $\alpha$ report few or no memberships across domains (i.e., $|A_i|\approx 0$, where $A_i$ is the set of domains in which individual $i$ holds membership). This position approximates a Community Lost configuration in the associational field because it offers limited exposure to organizational settings that could sustain either repeated interaction or cross-domain contact.

\textbf{Position $\beta$ (Clustering)} corresponds to embeddedness that is concentrated within a bounded domain. Individuals in $\beta$ report one or more memberships, but these memberships are largely confined to a single domain (i.e., $|A_i|>0$ but $C_i\approx 1$, where $C_i$ is the number of distinct domains represented in $i$'s portfolio). This position approximates a Community Saved configuration because it sustains repeated interaction and closure within a familiar social circle, which should be more conducive to localized trust than to generalized trust.

\textbf{Position $\gamma$ (Bridging)} corresponds to cross-domain embeddedness. Individuals in $\gamma$ report memberships that span multiple domains (i.e., $|A_i|>1$ and $C_i>1$), creating the potential for cross-cutting exposure across organizational settings. This position approximates a Community Liberated configuration because it links otherwise separated domains through co-membership and is therefore expected to be especially relevant for generalized trust. In network terms, $\gamma$ captures a brokerage-like role across domains \parencite{burt_brokerage_2005}.

To summarize breadth of participation, we use a domain-diversity measure $D_i = C_i/C_{\max}$, where $C_{\max}$ is the maximum number of domains observed in the data. This quantity helps validate that the empirically identified positions differ in cross-domain exposure, which is the core theoretical dimension linking portfolios to trust outcomes.





\subsection{Beyond Additive Approaches: The Configuration Problem}

These positions highlight a limitation of additive approaches to civic participation. When engagement is summarized as the number of memberships, portfolios that differ in cross-domain breadth can appear equivalent even though they imply different forms of exposure and interaction. A configuration perspective instead treats the patterning of memberships across domains as the relevant explanatory object for trust outcomes \parencite{putnam_bowling_2000, coleman_social_1988}.

\subsection{Theoretical Contributions}

Our framework makes three contributions to research on voluntary associations and trust formation. First, we shift the explanatory focus from participation count to portfolio configurations that place individuals in different positions within associational fields. While prior research relies on additive measures such as membership count or diversity indices \parencite{glanville_voluntary_2004, paxton_association_2007}. We instead argue that the configuration of membership matters. Specifically, cross-domain portfolios should be more strongly associated with generalized trust than portfolios concentrated within a single domain, even at comparable levels of participation intensity.

This portfolio-based approach differs from organization-centered strategies that classify associations by shared membership \parencite{paxton_association_2007}. Rather than using co-membership to characterize organizations, use duality logic to classify individuals based on the composition of their memberships. This distinction matters because the trust implications of a given association depend on how it is combined with others. Cross-domain portfolios expose individuals to heterogeneous organizational settings, facilitating the extension of trust expectations beyond familiar circles, whereas within-domain clustering reinforces bounded trust. A configuration perspective, therefore, predicts heterogeneity in trust outcomes that participation counts alone cannot detect.

Second, we introduce the concept of structural precarity to explain why the portfolio positions most conducive to generalized trust may be difficult to sustain. Cross-domain participation requires coordinating multiple commitments across distinct organizational routines, increasing time and cognitive demands. We therefore expect bridging portfolios (Position $\gamma$) to exhibit lower persistence over time than clustered portfolios (Position $\beta$), particularly under tighter resource constraints. Formally, this implies lower transition stability for bridging positions $P(\gamma_{t+1}\mid \gamma_t) < P(\beta_{t+1}\mid \beta_t)$, with resource constraints widening this gap. This claim is testable in a panel design because it concerns the transition probabilities rather than static cross-sectional differences.

Third, we describe a structural transposition process linking individual portfolio change to the integrative capacity of the associational field. When individuals exit bridging portfolios, cross-domain co-memberships decline, potentially narrowing opportunities for heterogeneous contact. This argument does not require formal inter-organizational ties; it only assumes that a subset of bridging individuals partly sustains cross-domain exposure. In bipartite terms, such exits alter the distribution of cross-domain co-memberships implied by $X$, with implications for field-level connectivity. Empirically, we focus on individual-level trust outcomes and position stability, while summarizing cross-domain co-membership patterns across waves to capture broader structural implications.


\subsection{Hypotheses}

The above arguments allow us to derive four hypotheses that link portfolio positions to trust outcomes and to their stability over time. For clarity, Position $\alpha$ denotes associational isolation, Position $\beta$ denotes within-domain clustering, and Position $\gamma$ denotes cross-domain bridging.


\textbf{H1 (Structural position effects).} Individuals in Position $\gamma$ (bridging) will exhibit higher generalized trust than individuals in Position $\beta$ (clustering) and Position $\alpha$ (isolation). This difference should be strongest when bridging portfolios span more distinct domains.

\textbf{H2 (Differential trust radii).} Position $\beta$ (clustering) will be more strongly associated with neighborhood trust than with generalized trust, whereas Position $\gamma$ (bridging) will be associated with both neighborhood trust and generalized trust.

\textbf{H3 (Structural precarity).} Position $\gamma$ (bridging) will exhibit lower temporal stability than Positions $\alpha$ and $\beta$, with higher exit probabilities among individuals facing greater resource constraints.

\textbf{H4 (Field-level implication).} Because cross-domain bridging is relatively rare, changes in the prevalence of Position $\gamma$ will coincide with changes in the extent of cross-domain co-membership implied by membership portfolios over time.


Together, these hypotheses summarize our core claim: trust formation depends on how memberships are configured across domains and on whether bridging portfolios can be maintained under unequal resource conditions.

\section{The Chilean Context}

Chile provides useful leverage for examining structural precarity in an unequal setting. The country combines a large associational infrastructure with persistently low interpersonal trust and comparatively high material inequality. With approximately 300{,}000 voluntary associations \parencite{irarrazaval_chile_2017} and a Gini coefficient of 44.9, Chile is a setting in which the opportunity costs of sustained cross-domain participation may be especially salient.

At the same time, observers have long noted fragmentation within Chilean civil society, including weak coordination across organizations operating in similar domains and limited pathways connecting grassroots groups to broader institutional arenas \parencite{programa_de_las_naciones_unidas_para_el_desarrollo_desarrollo_2000}. In such a context, cross-domain integration is more likely to depend on individuals who span domains than on stable organizational linkages.

Economic insecurity can also make sustained civic involvement appear costly relative to immediate demands, a condition that should amplify structural precarity for cross-domain portfolios. As \textcite{oxhorn_neopluralism_2004} argues, participation may be experienced as ``luxurious'' under insecurity, which implies that the ability to maintain bridging positions is likely stratified. This connects to broader arguments that access to social capital is itself unequally distributed \parencite{portes_social_1998}.

Together, these features make Chile a plausible setting in which associational abundance can coexist with limited cross-domain integration and low generalized trust. The Chilean case therefore helps illustrate how the configuration and stability of multiple memberships may condition whether civil society participation translates into trust beyond familiar circles.


\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Analytical Strategy}

Our empirical strategy links the theoretical framework to the data in three steps. First, we use longitudinal membership portfolios to estimate latent positions that summarize how individuals are embedded across association domains. Specifically, we estimate a latent Markov model using the eight binary membership indicators to classify individuals into positions corresponding to $\alpha$ (isolation), $\beta$ (clustering), and $\gamma$ (bridging). The model allows individuals to transition between positions across waves, consistent with our dynamic framework. We select the number of positions using information criteria and assess classification quality using standard diagnostics (e.g., posterior probabilities and entropy).

Second, we examine the temporal dynamics of these positions to assess structural precarity. Using the estimated transition matrix, we quantify persistence and movement between positions across waves. We test structural precarity by assessing whether persistence in Position $\gamma$ is lower than in alternative positions and by examining whether exits from $\gamma$ are more common among individuals with fewer socioeconomic resources (e.g., education and employment status).

Third, we relate time-varying position membership to generalized and neighborhood trust in panel models. Because both outcomes are binary and unobserved individual characteristics may correlate with associational involvement and trust, we estimate random-effects specifications as our baseline approach and assess robustness using correlated random-effects models that relax strict exogeneity assumptions. All models include observed covariates and wave fixed effects. This design allows us to evaluate both the stability of portfolio positions and their distinct implications for generalized versus localized trust.


\subsection{Data}

\subsubsection{Data Source and Sample}

We use the Chilean Longitudinal Social Survey (ELSOC), a nationally representative panel of Chilean adults in urban areas conducted by the Center for Social Conflict and Cohesion Studies (COES). Although ELSOC is fielded annually, we focus on the three waves (2016, 2018, 2022) in which associational membership items are consistently available and comparable.

Our analytical sample includes 1{,}304 respondents observed in all three waves, yielding a balanced panel. The retention rate over this period is 65.2\%. We assess whether attrition is systematically related to key baseline characteristics and treat remaining limitations as part of the study's observational scope.

The waves are spaced two to four years apart, which limits inference about short-term fluctuations but allows us to characterize medium-term changes in portfolio positions and their association with trust. All analyses use the same respondents across waves to isolate within-sample dynamics from compositional change due to refreshment samples.

\subsubsection{Measurement}


Following the theoretical distinction between generalized and localized trust, we measure two trust outcomes in each wave using standard survey items. Generalized social trust is measured with the standard item asking whether ``most people can be trusted'' versus ``you need to be very careful.'' We code the outcome as $1$ if respondents choose the trust response and $0$ otherwise. This measure captures trust toward unknown others, which our framework expects to be more closely linked to cross-domain participation.

Neighborhood trust is measured with an item about trusting one's neighbors. We construct a binary indicator equal to $1$ when respondents report trusting most in their neighbors and $0$ otherwise. This measure captures localized trust within a spatially bounded reference group, providing a contrast to generalized trust and allowing us to assess whether portfolio configurations differentially relate to distinct trust domains.

We measure the associational field through eight membership domains observed consistently in ELSOC: neighborhood organizations, religious organizations, political parties, labor unions, professional organizations, charitable organizations, sports organizations, and student organizations. For each domain, we use a binary indicator of membership in each wave.

\subsection{Stage I: Structural Position Identification}

\subsubsection{Latent Markov Model Specification}

We use a latent Markov model to summarize longitudinal membership portfolios into a small number of unobserved positions. The model treats the observed pattern of domain memberships in each wave as an indicator of a latent state that can change over time according to a first-order Markov process. This approach is well suited to our setting because it jointly captures (i) heterogeneity in portfolio types and (ii) transitions between types across waves.

Let $t=1,\ldots,T$ index survey waves and let $\mathbf{Y}^{(t)}=(Y^{(t)}_{1},\ldots,Y^{(t)}_{8})$ denote the vector of binary indicators for membership in the eight association domains in wave $t$. We assume that these indicators are conditionally independent given a latent state $U^{(t)}\in\{1,\ldots,K\}$, where each state represents a portfolio position.

\begin{equation}
f(\mathbf{y}) \;=\; \sum_{u_1=1}^{K} \cdots \sum_{u_T=1}^{K}
\pi_{u_1}\left(\prod_{t=2}^{T}\pi_{u_t\mid u_{t-1}}\right)
\left(\prod_{t=1}^{T}\prod_{j=1}^{8}\phi_{j\, y_j^{(t)}\mid u_t}\right),
\end{equation}

where $\pi_{u_1}$ denotes the initial-state probability, $\pi_{u_t\mid u_{t-1}}$ the transition probability from state $u_{t-1}$ to $u_t$, and $\phi_{j\, y_j^{(t)}\mid u_t}$ the conditional response probability for domain $j$ given state $u_t$.

To account for observed heterogeneity, we allow baseline covariates to predict (i) initial-state membership and (ii) transition probabilities via multinomial logit parameterizations. In the baseline specification, covariates include age, gender, and education; we also consider employment status in models that test resource constraints in transition dynamics.

We select the number of latent states $K$ using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), which is commonly preferred for finite-mixture models. We compare solutions with $K=1,\ldots,5$ and prioritize parsimony alongside fit. We report model-fit statistics and classification diagnostics (average posterior probabilities and entropy) to document separation between states.

\subsubsection{Empirical Identification of Wellman's Configurations}

We interpret the estimated states using the pattern of conditional response probabilities across domains. A three-state solution is theoretically interpretable as (i) isolation ($\alpha$), with uniformly low membership probabilities; (ii) clustering ($\beta$), with memberships concentrated within one or two domains; and (iii) bridging ($\gamma$), with nontrivial membership probabilities across multiple domains. We treat these labels as empirical summaries that are consistent with Wellman's Lost/Saved/Liberated configurations, rather than as direct measures of community structure.

As validation, we report classification quality (posterior probabilities and entropy) and examine whether covariate patterns align with theoretical expectations about resource requirements for bridging versus clustering. These checks do not establish causality, but they help assess whether the latent states capture substantively meaningful portfolio differences.


\subsection{Stage II: Temporal Dynamics Analysis}

We assess temporal dynamics using the estimated transition matrix, which summarizes the probability of moving from position $u$ at wave $t$ to position $v$ at wave $t+1$. We focus on persistence probabilities and the most common transition pathways to evaluate whether bridging ($\gamma$) is less stable than alternative positions, as predicted by structural precarity.

To test the resource-constraint component of structural precarity, we examine whether exits from $\gamma$ are patterned by socioeconomic characteristics. In practice, we evaluate whether lower education and weaker labor-market attachment are associated with higher probabilities of transitioning out of bridging, conditional on prior position.

Where possible, we summarize field-level implications of these transitions by tracking how cross-domain co-membership implied by portfolios varies across waves. Because bridging portfolios are relatively rare, changes in the prevalence of $\gamma$ can coincide with changes in cross-domain connectivity in the associational field. We treat this as a descriptive implication of portfolio dynamics rather than as a causal estimate of system-level change.

\subsection{Stage III: Trust Formation Analysis}

\subsubsection{Model Specification}

We estimate panel models relating time-varying position membership to trust outcomes. Let $\text{trust}_{it}$ denote either generalized trust or neighborhood trust for individual $i$ at wave $t$. Our baseline specification is a random-effects probit model,

\begin{equation}
P(\text{trust}_{it}=1) \;=\; \Phi\!\left(\kappa
+ \theta_{\beta}\,\mathbbm{1}\{\text{Position}_{it}=\beta\}
+ \theta_{\gamma}\,\mathbbm{1}\{\text{Position}_{it}=\gamma\}
+ \mathbf{X}_{it}\boldsymbol{\lambda}
+ \delta_t + a_i\right),
\end{equation}

where $\mathbbm{1}\{\cdot\}$ is an indicator function, $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ includes demographic and socioeconomic controls, $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ is a vector of coefficients, $\delta_t$ are wave fixed effects, and $a_i\sim N(0,\sigma_a^2)$ captures time-invariant individual heterogeneity.

We estimate separate models for generalized trust and neighborhood trust to assess differential trust radii. The control vector includes subjective well-being, employment status, and couple status. Demographic variables (age, sex, education) enter the LMM as predictors of latent class membership and are therefore excluded from the trust models to avoid double-counting. We report results as average marginal effects (AMEs) to facilitate interpretation in percentage-point changes.

This stage evaluates the hypotheses linking portfolio positions to trust outcomes. In particular, we test whether bridging ($\gamma$) is more strongly associated with generalized trust than clustering ($\beta$) and whether clustering is more closely associated with neighborhood trust than with generalized trust. Given low baseline generalized trust in Chile, we interpret AMEs in absolute percentage points and, where helpful, in relative terms.

Because position membership and trust may share unobserved determinants, we interpret the estimates as associations. The panel structure, wave fixed effects, and observed covariates reduce but do not eliminate concerns about selection and time-varying confounding. We therefore complement baseline specifications with robustness checks that assess sensitivity to alternative assumptions about unobserved heterogeneity and classification uncertainty.

Latent Markov models are estimated by maximum likelihood using the EM algorithm (LMest in R). Trust models are estimated as random-effects probit models with clustered standard errors at the individual level; survey weights are applied where appropriate. Full implementation details and additional specifications are reported in the Supplementary Appendix.

Two limitations are worth noting. First, the observational design limits causal claims despite longitudinal structure and covariate adjustment. Second, the spacing between waves may miss short-lived changes in participation, and binary trust items may not capture the full complexity of trust orientations. We treat these limitations as scope conditions when interpreting the results.

We interpret the three latent states based on their conditional membership probabilities across the eight domains Figure \ref{fig:profiles}. The profiles reveal clear differences in both the level and dispersion of associational involvement.

Position $\alpha$ (55\% of respondents) displays uniformly low membership probabilities across all domains, none exceeding 0.17. The flat and compressed profile indicates not only low intensity of participation but also minimal cross-domain exposure. This configuration is consistent with associational isolation: individuals in this state are unlikely to be embedded in any sustained organizational environment, limiting opportunities for either localized reinforcement or cross-domain interaction.

Position $\beta$ (34\%) concentrates membership in a small set of bounded domains. Membership probabilities are high in neighborhood organizations (0.61) and religious organizations (0.64), while remaining low in all other domains (generally below 0.15). Individuals in this state are clearly active, but their participation is bounded within socially proximate and often overlapping spheres. This profile is therefore consistent with within-domain clustering: relatively high involvement combined with limited cross-domain breadth.

Position $\gamma$ (11\%) is characterized by dispersion rather than concentration. Membership probabilities are elevated across multiple domains, including neighborhood organizations (0.62), charitable organizations (0.53), professional associations (0.46), unions (0.38), and political parties. Although probabilities are not uniformly high, the profiles lack the sharp concentration observed in $\beta$ and instead shows sustained participation across heterogeneous organizational spheres. This broader spread is consistent with cross-domain bridging, as individuals in this state are simultaneously embedded in different organizations.

Given the above, we observe that latent states differ not simply in overall participation levels but in the configuration of memberships. Position $\alpha$ reflects generalized disengagement, Position $\beta$ reflects bounded clustering within a narrow segment of the association field, and Position $\gamma$ reflects structurally dispersed involvement across multiple domains.

Two checks support the substantive interpretability of the positions. First, classification quality is acceptable: average posterior probabilities exceed 0.75 and entropy is 0.68, indicating meaningful separation between states. \section{Discussion and Conclusions}

This study revisits the long-standing question of whether and when associational life fosters trust. Existing literature argues that voluntary associations foster generalized trust by exposing individuals to diverse people and norms of reciprocity \parencite{paxton_social_2002,paxton_trust_2018}. Yet, empirical evidence is mixed, particularly when participation is measured as simple counts of memberships. We argue that this ambiguity stems in part from treating participation as simple aggregate rather than as a structural aspect. Using longitudinal data from Chile, we identify three portfolio positions—isolation ($\alpha$), clustering ($\beta$), and bridging ($\gamma$)—and show that these positions differ in both their association with trust and their temporal stability. Trust is highest in $\gamma$, intermediate in $\beta$, and lowest in $\alpha$, with the largest separation for generalized trust. At the same time, $\gamma$ is the least stable position, and exits from $\gamma$ are socially patterned in ways consistent with resource constraints. The central implication is structural: the portfolio position most compatible with generalized trust is also the hardest to maintain under unequal conditions, limiting the extent to which organizational abundance translates into durable cross-domain integration.

A first contribution is conceptual and measurement-focused: we translate claims about cross-cutting affiliations into an individual-level portfolio framework that is observable in standard survey data. Rather than treating “community” as a single condition, the portfolio approach distinguishes positions that differ in cross-domain exposure and, therefore, in the kinds of trust they plausibly support. This helps clarify why participation counts can be weak predictors of generalized trust \parencite{paxton_association_2007}. Counts collapse substantively different portfolios into a single metric and obscure whether individuals are embedded within one domain or spanning several. By shifting attention from the quantity of memberships to their configuration, the framework provides a tractable way to test when associational life is consistent with trust generalization.

A second contribution is substantive: portfolio positions align with different trust radii. Bridging portfolios ($\gamma$) are associated with higher generalized trust, consistent with the idea that exposure across organizational settings can make cooperative expectations more portable beyond familiar circles. Clustering portfolios ($\beta$) show a weaker pattern for generalized trust and a more comparable pattern for neighborhood trust, consistent with bounded interaction and closure supporting localized cooperation \parencite{coleman_social_1988,portes_social_1998}. The broader implication is that “civic participation” is not uniformly trust-producing; the same number of memberships can imply different social experiences depending on whether they are concentrated or cross-domain.

A third contribution is dynamic: we propose and test structural precarity as a constraint on cross-domain integration. Bridging portfolios are markedly less stable over time than isolation and clustering, and exits from $\gamma$ are stratified by socioeconomic resources. This pattern implies a structural tension in unequal contexts: the portfolio position most compatible with generalized trust is also the one that is hardest to maintain. Even when civil society offers many organizational options, unequal resources can limit who sustains cross-domain commitments, reducing the durability of cross-cutting exposure in the population. In this sense, the civil society–trust gap can reflect not only low participation, but also the fragility of the configurations most relevant for generalized trust.

These findings should be interpreted with appropriate caution. The design is observational, and unobserved traits may shape both portfolio position and trust. The panel structure, wave indicators, and covariate adjustment reduce—but do not eliminate—selection concerns, and the spacing between waves limits inference about short-lived changes in participation. In addition, membership is self-reported and does not capture intensity of involvement, which may matter for trust formation. We therefore interpret the estimates as associations that are consistent with the proposed mechanisms rather than as definitive causal effects.

The results suggest clear directions for future work. First, research on associations and trust should move beyond participation counts toward models that represent portfolio structure and its dynamics. Second, stronger designs are needed to address selection and isolate the role of exposure diversity—for example, interventions that facilitate participation in single-domain versus cross-domain settings, or quasi-experimental changes that shift the costs of sustained involvement. Third, direct measures of the maintenance costs that motivate structural precarity—time constraints, caregiving burdens, transportation, and economic insecurity—would allow sharper tests of why individuals exit bridging positions. Finally, comparative extensions would clarify scope conditions by testing whether the stability of bridging portfolios varies with inequality and with institutional supports that lower participation costs.

The policy implication is conditional rather than sweeping: civil society may matter for trust, but its trust-generating potential depends on the feasibility of maintaining cross-domain engagement. Interventions that only increase participation rates may have limited effects if they do not reduce the opportunity costs that make bridging portfolios fragile. Policies that lower barriers to sustained involvement—such as flexible schedules, childcare support, or reduced transportation burdens—may increase not only participation but also the durability of the cross-domain portfolios most relevant for generalized trust. Moreover, the implications extend beyond trust. Membership in voluntary associations has been linked to broader spillover effects, including enhanced economic performance, information diffusion, and cooperative behavior in collective action settings \parencite{audia_effect_2010}.

In sum, the findings support a structural account of trust formation under inequality. Civil society can provide settings for localized cooperation and for cross-cutting exposure, but the latter depends on membership configurations that are unevenly accessible and temporally fragile. By modeling membership portfolios as positions that evolve over time, the study links individual engagement patterns to the distribution and stability of cross-domain exposure in the population. Understanding when bridging portfolios can be created and sustained is therefore central to explaining when associational life is associated with generalized trust.
